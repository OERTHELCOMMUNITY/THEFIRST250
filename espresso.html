What is espresso, really? The complete guide.

Everything about espresso in one place: what it is, where it comes from, how it's made, how much caffeine it contains, why it feels different from an energy drink, and when to drink it for maximum effect. Written for people who want the real answer, not marketing.

By the OERTHEL Coffee Team · 18 min read · Last updated July 2026 · Fact-checked against EFSA & peer-reviewed pharmacology sources
Quick answer

Espresso is a concentrated coffee brew made by forcing hot water at roughly 9 bars of pressure through finely-ground, tightly-packed coffee. A standard single shot is about 25–30ml, contains on average 63–80mg of caffeine, and is topped with a layer of golden-brown foam called crema. It's the base for cappuccino, latte, macchiato and flat white, and — unlike energy drinks — contains no sugar, no synthetic caffeine, and no artificial additives.

What is espresso?

Espresso is a brewing method, not a bean or a roast. The name comes from the Italian word for "pressed" or "expressed" — a reference to the way hot water is forced through the coffee under pressure, rather than simply poured or steeped over it like filter coffee. That single mechanical difference is what defines everything else about espresso: its strength, its texture, its speed, and its signature crema.

The technical definition

A true espresso is produced when water heated to roughly 90–96°C is pushed through 7–9 grams of finely-ground coffee at approximately 9 bars of pressure, extracting a 25–30ml shot in about 25–30 seconds. Change any one of these variables — grind size, pressure, dose, or time — and you no longer have an espresso; you have something else entirely.

9 bar Standard extraction pressure — roughly 9x atmospheric pressure
25–30s Ideal extraction time for a balanced shot
93°C Typical water temperature for optimal extraction

What is crema, and why does it matter?

Crema is the reddish-brown foam that sits on top of a properly pulled shot. It forms because the high pressure of extraction emulsifies the coffee's natural oils and dissolves carbon dioxide into microscopic bubbles. Crema isn't just decorative — it's a reliable visual indicator of a fresh, well-extracted shot made from properly roasted beans. Flat, thin, or absent crema usually signals stale beans or an incorrect brew.

In short: espresso is defined by pressure, not by bean origin or roast level. Any coffee bean can technically be turned into an espresso — but the grind, freshness, and roast profile determine whether the result is excellent or bitter.

Where does espresso come from?

Espresso was born in Italy at the turn of the 20th century, driven by a simple commercial problem: brewing coffee the traditional way took several minutes per cup, which limited how many customers a café bar could serve.

From steam pressure to the modern lever machine

In 1901, Milanese engineer Luigi Bezzera patented an early steam-driven machine that forced hot water through coffee grounds far faster than any existing method. Desiderio Pavoni later commercialized the design. But it was Achille Gaggia who, in 1938, introduced the piston lever mechanism that finally achieved true high-pressure extraction — and with it, the first crema ever seen on a cup of coffee. This is widely considered the true birth of modern espresso as we know it today.

By the 1950s and 60s, espresso bars had spread across Italy and into the rest of Europe, becoming a cultural fixture: a fast, standing ritual rather than a slow, seated one. Espresso's global expansion accelerated dramatically from the 1980s onward, driven by the rise of specialty coffee chains that exported the Italian bar format worldwide — while adapting it into milk-based drinks like the latte and cappuccino that most people now recognize first.

Why this matters for flavor: understanding espresso's Italian bar-culture origin explains why a "correct" espresso is small, intense, and quick to drink — not a large, milky, sweetened beverage. Everything else (cappuccino, latte, flat white) is a variation built on top of that original concentrated shot.

How is espresso made?

Making espresso is simple in principle and unforgiving in practice. Four variables have to align almost perfectly, which is why the same beans can taste completely different depending on who pulls the shot.

  1. Grind: Beans are ground extremely fine — finer than for any other brew method — to create enough resistance for the water to build pressure as it passes through.
  2. Dose & tamp: Roughly 7–9 grams of grounds are distributed evenly and compressed ("tamped") into a portafilter basket to ensure water flows through evenly, not just along the path of least resistance.
  3. Extraction: The machine forces water at ~9 bars of pressure and ~93°C through the puck for 25–30 seconds, dissolving soluble oils, sugars, and acids into a concentrated shot.
  4. Result: A 25–30ml shot with a layer of crema, balancing bitterness, acidity, and sweetness depending on the bean's roast and origin.

Cold brew espresso: the modern alternative

A newer method — cold brew espresso — steeps coarsely-ground coffee in cold or room-temperature water for extended periods, often 12–18 hours, instead of using heat and pressure. This slow extraction pulls fewer bitter compounds and acids from the beans, producing a naturally smoother, less acidic concentrate that still delivers a comparable caffeine punch to traditional espresso. It's this method that makes canned, ready-to-drink espresso — like OERTHEL — possible without sacrificing flavor or freshness.

Why is espresso so popular?

Espresso's popularity isn't only about taste or caffeine — it's about ritual, identity, and the psychology of small, repeatable habits.

Ritual and routine

Humans are wired to find comfort in predictable sequences. A morning espresso is rarely just about the caffeine; it's a fixed marker that separates "before" from "after," a small ceremony that signals the start of focus or the start of a break. This is the same psychological mechanism behind habit loops: cue, routine, reward.

Social proof and identity

Ordering "just an espresso" at a bar carries a certain cultural shorthand — it signals familiarity with coffee culture. That subtle social signaling, combined with the sheer global visibility of espresso-based drinks in cafés everywhere, reinforces its status as the default reference point for "real" coffee.

Speed meets intensity

Because a shot takes seconds to drink and delivers a concentrated dose of caffeine, espresso fits naturally into modern, time-poor lifestyles in a way that a slow-brewed pot of filter coffee does not.

The dopamine loop behind "just one more"

Caffeine doesn't directly flood the brain with dopamine the way some stimulants do, but it does enhance dopamine signaling by increasing the sensitivity of dopamine receptors, particularly in brain regions tied to motivation and reward. Combined with the immediate sensory reward of aroma, warmth, and bitterness balanced by sweetness, this creates a genuinely reinforcing loop — one reason a daily espresso can feel less like a beverage choice and more like an anticipated event.

Scarcity and FOMO in coffee culture

Limited-edition roasts, single-origin drops, and "first access" launches tap into the same psychological trigger as any limited product: the fear of missing out. Specialty coffee culture has leaned into this deliberately, turning beans and even ready-to-drink formats into something people want to try before a batch runs out — a dynamic that mirrors how streetwear or sneaker drops operate, just applied to coffee.

Why do so many people find espresso delicious?

Flavor in espresso comes down to chemistry that happens long before the shot is ever pulled: the roasting process.

The Maillard reaction and caramelization

When green coffee beans are roasted, heat triggers the Maillard reaction — the same browning reaction responsible for the flavor of toasted bread and seared meat — along with caramelization of natural sugars. Together, these reactions create hundreds of aromatic compounds responsible for espresso's notes of chocolate, nuts, caramel, and toasted bread.

Concentration amplifies flavor

Because espresso uses far less water relative to coffee than filter brewing, it captures a much higher concentration of these aromatic compounds and oils per sip. That's why an espresso can taste sweeter and richer than a filter coffee made from the exact same beans, despite containing zero added sugar.

Interesting detail: the fat-soluble compounds captured in crema are largely responsible for the "body" or mouthfeel of espresso — the reason it feels heavier and more textured on the tongue than filtered coffee.

Why does caffeine actually work?

To understand why espresso feels energizing, you need to understand what caffeine does — and, just as importantly, what it doesn't do.

Caffeine blocks tiredness. It doesn't create energy.

Throughout the day, a molecule called adenosine gradually builds up in the brain, binding to adenosine receptors and producing the sensation of "sleep pressure" — the growing feeling of drowsiness that peaks in the evening. Caffeine has a molecular structure similar enough to adenosine that it can occupy the same receptors without activating them, a process known as receptor antagonism. With those receptors blocked, the brain simply doesn't receive the "get tired" signal for a while — it isn't gaining energy, it's masking fatigue.

How long does it last?

Caffeine typically reaches peak blood concentration within 15 to 120 minutes of consumption, and its half-life — the time it takes for the body to clear half of it — averages around 4 to 6 hours in a healthy adult, though this varies significantly based on genetics, liver enzyme activity, pregnancy, and medication use such as hormonal contraceptives.

15–120min Time to peak blood concentration after drinking
4–6 hrs Average half-life in a healthy adult
200mg EFSA-recognised safe single dose for healthy adults

This is also why caffeine consumed in the afternoon can still disrupt sleep at night — a large share of it may still be circulating well past dinner time, quietly keeping adenosine receptors occupied.

Espresso vs. energy drink

This is one of the most common comparisons people search for — and one of the most misunderstood. Both promise energy and alertness, but the source of that effect, and what comes with it, are very different.

The key distinction is the sugar spike. Energy drinks that contain 25–30 grams of sugar cause a rapid rise and subsequent fall in blood glucose, which is widely associated with the sudden "crash" many people report an hour or two after drinking one. Espresso, having zero sugar, avoids that particular mechanism entirely — any dip in alertness afterward comes purely from caffeine clearing the system, not from a glucose rebound.

Espresso vs. filter coffee, cappuccino and latte

"Coffee" is a category; espresso, filter coffee, cappuccino and latte are all specific preparations within it. Here's how they actually differ.

Espresso vs. filter coffee

Filter coffee uses gravity, not pressure, and a much longer contact time between water and grounds — typically 4–6 minutes rather than 25–30 seconds. This produces a larger, lighter-bodied cup with a milder, less concentrated flavor. Per volume, espresso has far more caffeine; per serving, a large mug of filter coffee can contain more total caffeine simply because the serving size is bigger.

Espresso vs. cappuccino and latte

Both cappuccino and latte start with the exact same base: one or two shots of espresso. The difference is entirely in the milk. A cappuccino uses roughly equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and milk foam, giving it a light, airy texture. A latte uses far more steamed milk and only a thin layer of foam, producing a milder, creamier drink. Neither adds caffeine beyond what the espresso shots already contain — the milk changes texture and taste, not stimulant strength.

How much caffeine is in espresso?

A single 25–30ml shot of espresso typically contains between 63 and 80mg of caffeine, though this varies with bean type, roast level, and dose. Robusta beans contain roughly double the caffeine of Arabica, so blends matter as much as shot size.

How much is safe?

According to the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), single doses of up to 200mg of caffeine and total daily intake of up to 400mg do not raise safety concerns for the general healthy adult population. For context, 200mg is roughly equivalent to two-and-a-half single espresso shots. Pregnant women are advised to stay under 200mg per day from all sources, and children and adolescents are advised to limit intake to around 3mg per kilogram of body weight.

Practical translation: a healthy adult could reasonably drink 4–5 single espressos across a full day and remain within EFSA's daily guidance — though individual sensitivity, sleep quality, and existing health conditions should always be taken into account.

Health effects: benefits and drawbacks of espresso

Espresso, consumed in moderation, is generally considered safe for most healthy adults and is associated with a range of documented effects — both positive and negative.

Potential benefits
  • Improved short-term alertness, reaction time and concentration
  • Rich source of antioxidant polyphenols from roasted coffee beans
  • Zero sugar when consumed black, unlike most sweetened alternatives
  • Associated in population studies with modestly improved metabolic markers when consumed in moderation
  • Can enhance physical performance when timed before exercise
Potential drawbacks
  • Can increase heart rate and blood pressure temporarily in sensitive individuals
  • May disrupt sleep if consumed within 6–8 hours of bedtime
  • Can cause jitteriness, anxiety or digestive discomfort at high doses
  • Regular heavy use can lead to physical dependence and withdrawal symptoms
  • Not recommended in high amounts during pregnancy

As with most dietary compounds, the effect is dose-dependent: the same cup that sharpens focus at 100–200mg can produce anxiety-like symptoms at much higher single doses, particularly in people with lower caffeine tolerance.

What about heart health, long term?

Large observational studies have generally found that moderate coffee consumption — including espresso-based drinks — is not associated with increased long-term cardiovascular risk in the general population, and some research even points to modestly lower rates of certain cardiovascular and metabolic conditions among moderate coffee drinkers compared to non-drinkers. This doesn't mean coffee is a treatment for anything; it means that, for most healthy adults, moderate daily intake appears to be neutral-to-favorable rather than harmful — a very different picture from the concerns historically raised about heavy energy drink consumption.

Is espresso addictive? How does caffeine dependence develop?

Caffeine is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance in the world, and regular use does produce genuine physical dependence — though it's important to distinguish this from the more severe dependence patterns associated with drugs like nicotine or alcohol.

How the dependence builds

With daily use, the brain adapts to the constant blocking of adenosine receptors by producing more of them — a process called receptor upregulation. Over time, this means more caffeine is needed to produce the same alerting effect, and when caffeine is suddenly withdrawn, the now-larger population of unblocked adenosine receptors triggers a rebound of symptoms.

Common withdrawal symptoms

  • Headache — the most commonly reported symptom, often starting 12–24 hours after the last dose
  • Fatigue and drowsiness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Irritability or low mood
  • Occasionally flu-like muscle stiffness or nausea

These symptoms typically peak within one to two days and resolve within about a week, which is broadly consistent with how quickly tolerance can also build back up — often within just a few days of resumed regular use.

The honest answer: espresso itself isn't addictive in a pharmacological sense the way nicotine is — the caffeine within it is what creates dependence, and it does so the same way regardless of whether it comes from espresso, filter coffee, tea, or a can.

The best time of day to drink espresso

Timing matters more than most people realize, largely because of a natural hormone called cortisol.

Why "first thing in the morning" isn't actually optimal

Cortisol — the body's natural alertness hormone — follows a daily rhythm and peaks shortly after waking, roughly between 8 and 9am for most people on a typical schedule. Drinking espresso during this natural cortisol peak means caffeine is competing with a hormone that's already doing the job of waking you up, which some researchers argue blunts its effect and accelerates tolerance. A commonly suggested alternative is to wait until 60–90 minutes after waking, once the natural cortisol peak has started to decline, so caffeine's effect stands out more clearly.

Why afternoon and evening espresso can backfire

Given caffeine's 4–6 hour average half-life, an espresso at 3pm can still leave meaningful amounts of caffeine circulating at 9pm or later. The American Academy of Sleep Medicine commonly recommends avoiding caffeine within 6–8 hours of your intended bedtime to protect deep sleep quality — a factor that itself strongly influences next-day alertness and, ironically, next-day caffeine "need."

Practical rule of thumb: if you go to bed around 11pm, treat roughly 3–5pm as your last reasonable caffeine cut-off, adjusting earlier if you're a slow caffeine metabolizer or sensitive sleeper.

Espresso for sport, work and studying

Espresso before exercise

Caffeine is one of the most well-studied ergogenic (performance-enhancing) compounds available, associated with improved endurance, power output and perceived exertion when consumed roughly 30–60 minutes before physical activity. EFSA has specifically noted that single doses of up to 200mg do not raise safety concerns even when consumed less than two hours before intense exercise in healthy adults.

Espresso at work: focus without the crash

For sustained knowledge work, the appeal of espresso over sugary alternatives is straightforward: it provides the adenosine-blocking alertness effect without the glucose spike-and-crash cycle that sugar-heavy drinks introduce. Many people find that spacing smaller, more frequent espresso shots across the day sustains focus more evenly than one large, sugary energy drink consumed all at once.

Espresso for students

Students frequently reach for whatever is cheapest and fastest — often that means sugary energy drinks consumed late into study sessions. The trade-off is real: the sugar crash that follows can undermine the very concentration the drink was meant to support, and late consumption directly interferes with the sleep that memory consolidation depends on. An unsweetened espresso, consumed earlier in the study session, targets the alertness benefit without either downside.

Frequently asked questions about espresso

Is espresso stronger than regular coffee?

Per milliliter, yes — espresso is far more concentrated. But per serving, a large mug of filter coffee can contain more total caffeine simply because its serving size is much bigger than a 30ml shot.

Does espresso have more caffeine than instant coffee?

Per volume, espresso is more concentrated. Per average serving size, the totals can be comparable, since instant coffee is typically consumed in much larger cups than a single espresso shot.

Can espresso be decaffeinated?

Yes. Decaffeinated beans go through a process that removes roughly 97% or more of their caffeine before roasting, and can be pulled as espresso using the exact same method and equipment.

Why does espresso sometimes taste sour?

Sourness usually indicates under-extraction — the shot ran too fast, the grind was too coarse, or the water wasn't hot enough, leaving acidic compounds under-dissolved relative to the sweeter, heavier compounds.

Is espresso bad for your stomach?

Espresso's natural acids and caffeine can trigger acid reflux or stomach discomfort in sensitive individuals, particularly on an empty stomach. This effect varies significantly from person to person.

Does milk reduce espresso's caffeine content?

No. Milk changes texture and flavor but adds no caffeine and removes none — a latte and a straight double espresso made from the same two shots contain the same amount of caffeine.

What is cold brew espresso in a can?

It's espresso-strength coffee extracted slowly at cold temperatures rather than with heat and pressure, then packaged ready to drink. It preserves natural caffeine and flavor without needing a machine — the approach used by OERTHEL.

Is espresso vegan and gluten-free?

Black espresso made from 100% coffee beans is naturally vegan and gluten-free. Milk-based drinks like cappuccino or latte are not vegan unless made with a plant-based milk alternative.

How is espresso different around the world?

In Italy, espresso is traditionally a short, intense shot drunk standing at a bar in seconds. Specialty coffee culture in Northern Europe, the US, and Australia has since built an entire menu of milk-based variations on top of that base shot — flat whites, oat-milk lattes, iced versions — while still relying on the same underlying extraction method.

Conclusion: espresso is chemistry, ritual, and choice

Espresso earns its place at the center of coffee culture through a genuinely rare combination: fast preparation, concentrated flavor built by real chemistry, and a stimulant effect people have relied on for over a century. Unlike sugar-laden energy drinks, its energizing effect comes from one ingredient doing one job — natural caffeine blocking adenosine receptors — with no sugar spike to crash from afterward.

Understanding how it works — the pressure that extracts it, the adenosine it blocks, the half-life that determines when to stop drinking it — turns espresso from a daily habit into an informed choice. Whether that's a shot pulled fresh at a machine, or a cold brew espresso poured straight from a can, the underlying science is the same.
